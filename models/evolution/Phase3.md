## Phase 1.3: The Piolín Optimization and Final WRO 2026 Architecture

Phase 3 represents the definitive culmination of our engineering efforts for the WRO 2026 Future Engineers competition. This phase marks a complete departure from the fragile experimental prototypes of our early development cycle. The primary objective defining Phase 3 is the establishment of absolute mechanical reliability and deterministic software execution. By centralizing our processing logic onto the LEGO Mindstorms EV3 platform, we have transformed the Piolín robot into a highly optimized, competition ready autonomous vehicle. This comprehensive documentation details every subsystem, architectural decision, and mathematical model that defines this final phase.

#### Architectural Shift and Engineering Philosophy

The transition into Phase 3 required a fundamental reevaluation of our computing and structural priorities. In previous phases, the pursuit of excessive computational power led to fragmented architectures that were highly susceptible to communication bottlenecks and hardware failures. The core philosophy of Phase 3 is robust autonomy through integration. We deliberately selected the EV3 Intelligent Brick to serve as the unified processing core. This platform provides a factory synchronized environment where motor feedback loops and internal bus timings operate with absolute predictability. In a competition environment where ambient lighting, track surface conditions, and physical vibrations constantly threaten stability, the EV3 isolates our control logic from the unpredictable operating system latencies common in general purpose microcomputers. This guarantees that our control algorithms execute precisely when expected.

#### Advanced Structural Dynamics and Materials

The mechanical foundation of Piolín underwent a rigorous redesign to eliminate the physical compliance that plagued earlier iterations. The chassis is a sophisticated hybrid construction that leverages the rapid assembly properties of LEGO Technic alongside the tailored rigidity of custom 3D printed components. Structural flex during high speed cornering alters the physical geometry of the robot, rendering software based steering corrections inaccurate. To solve this, Phase 3 introduces a heavily cross braced frame design.

A major breakthrough in our mechanical reliability involved the correction of our steering knuckle assembly. We identified and implemented the correct structural components, specifically relying on a crucial Technic piece that features exactly 3 holes: two horizontal and one vertical. The precise geometry of this piece provides the necessary mounting points to lock the steering kingpin into a rigid upright position. By utilizing this specific 3 hole component rather than the incorrect 2 hole or 4 hole variants, we eliminated the torsional twisting that previously degraded our steering response.

Furthermore, we integrated custom 3D printed bevel gears and reinforced chassis connectors. These custom components interface perfectly with the 3 hole Technic pieces to create a steering linkage with zero mechanical slop. When the steering servo actuates, the mechanical force translates directly into wheel rotation without being absorbed by frame bending. This absolute physical predictability allows our software control loops to operate on a consistent baseline.

#### Electrical Integration and Motor Configuration

The electrical architecture in Phase 3 prioritizes signal integrity and power stability. The system routes all peripheral data and motor commands through the shielded, standardized ports of the EV3 brick. This eliminates the chaotic wiring harnesses and fragile breadboard connections that caused intermittent failures in the past.

A critical update in Phase 3 is the finalized motor driver configuration. Extensive testing and debugging led us to correct our motor pin mapping. The drive and steering motors are now strictly configured to operate using the ain1 and ain2 pins. We completely discarded the problematic bin1 and bin2 configuration, which had previously caused unpredictable torque delivery and directionality errors. This verified ain1 and ain2 mapping ensures that the high frequency PWM signals generated by the EV3 translate smoothly into mechanical rotation.

Additionally, the system stability is strictly managed by controlling the hardware state of the EV3 brick itself. To eliminate potential input and output bottlenecks that can occur when reading from external memory, we have verified that the EV3 operates entirely from its internal memory structure. There is absolutely no micro SD card connected to the EV3 during operational track runs. This guarantees that the operating system never pauses a critical steering calculation to wait for an external memory read cycle.

#### Optical Navigation via PixyCam 2.1

The vision system is the primary navigational instrument for Piolín. We resolved earlier hardware misidentifications to confirm that the PixyCam 2.1 is the exclusive optical sensor utilized in this architecture. The PixyCam functions as a dedicated vision coprocessor, utilizing an NXP LPC4330 dual core processor to handle all image parsing.

The sensor employs a Color Connected Components algorithm. It captures raw light data and immediately converts it into the Hue, Saturation, and Value color space. This conversion is critical because Hue remains relatively stable even when the intensity of the track lighting fluctuates. The PixyCam identifies pixels that match our pre taught color signatures and groups them into bounding boxes.

The camera then calculates the center coordinates of these boxes and transmits this lightweight data packet to the EV3 over the I2C protocol. This offloads the immense computational burden of video processing from the EV3, allowing the main brick to focus entirely on maintaining high frequency steering and speed control loops. To protect the sensor from overhead venue glare, we integrated a modular physical shroud that acts as an aperture, restricting incoming light to the track surface directly ahead of the robot.

#### Ultrasonic Telemetry and Sensor Fusion

To complement the optical navigation system, Phase 3 incorporates a highly responsive spatial awareness array using three HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors. This array provides a 180 degree protective envelope around the front and flanks of the vehicle.

The integration of these sensors is managed through dedicated EV3 sensor ports. Because ultrasonic sound waves can bounce off track seams or floor imperfections to create ghost echoes, our software applies a moving average mathematical filter to the incoming distance data. This ensures the robot only reacts to solid, verified obstacles.

This multi sensor approach enables true sensor fusion. The robot utilizes the PixyCam data for global path planning and the ultrasonic data for immediate tactical evasion. If the camera detects a clear path but the inner flank ultrasonic sensor detects an obstacle in the blind spot, the control logic triggers an immediate priority override. The robot will adjust its steering angle to clear the obstacle before returning to the visually plotted center line, ensuring the chassis remains protected from physical collisions at all times.

#### Mathematical Control Models

The autonomy of Piolín is governed by precise mathematical frameworks implemented within the EV3 software environment. The primary mechanism for path adherence is the Proportional Integral Derivative control loop.

The PID loop calculates the necessary steering correction based on the error detected by the PixyCam. We define the steering error $e(t)$ as the difference between the target frame center and the actual coordinate of the detected color signature. The control output $u(t)$ is calculated using the standard PID equation:


$$u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int_{0}^{t} e(\tau) d\tau + K_d \frac{de(t)}{dt}$$

The proportional term $K_p$ provides the immediate restorative force. The integral term $K_i$ accumulates the error to correct for long term drift on sustained curves. The derivative term $K_d$ predicts future error to damp the motion, completely eliminating the violent zig zag oscillations seen in earlier phases.

To execute this steering command physically, we utilize the Ackermann steering geometry model. This ensures that the inner and outer wheels turn at different angles to track concentric circles, preventing tire scrub. The required steering angle $\delta$ for a target radius $R$ and a specific wheelbase $L$ is calculated as:


$$\delta = \arctan\left(\frac{L}{R}\right)$$

Finally, the obstacle detection logic calculates the physical distance $d$ based on the time of flight $t$ of the ultrasonic pulse and the speed of sound $v$:


$$d = \frac{t \cdot v}{2}$$

#### Asynchronous Software Architecture

The final major leap in Phase 3 is the implementation of a fully asynchronous software architecture. Earlier phases relied on blocking code, meaning the robot would pause its steering calculations while waiting for a camera frame or a sensor ping. This caused stuttering movement and slow reaction times.

The new control software separates the vision parsing, the ultrasonic pinging, and the motor control into distinct execution threads that run concurrently on the EV3. This guarantees that the PID control loop evaluates the steering error at a constant, high frequency rate, regardless of how long the PixyCam takes to report a new signature. This asynchronous flow allows Piolín to process complex environmental data while maintaining a perfectly fluid driving trajectory.

Phase 3 has transformed Piolín from a collection of parts into a unified, competition ready robotic platform. Every element of the vehicle has been subjected to extreme scrutiny and optimization. The structural frame is rigid and mechanically sound, relying on precise 3 hole Technic pieces and custom gearing. The electrical system is stable, utilizing the correct ain1 and ain2 motor pins without the bottleneck of an SD card. The vision system is perfectly tuned to the PixyCam 2.1, and the mathematical control loops operate flawlessly within an asynchronous software environment. This careful engineering ensures that Piolín is fully prepared to execute complex autonomous navigation tasks on the WRO 2026 track with maximum speed and reliability.
